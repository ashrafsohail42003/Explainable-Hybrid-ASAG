\begin{abstract}
Automatic short answer grading (ASAG) systems are increasingly accurate but
opaque, and their reported accuracy is often inflated by an under-examined
evaluation artifact: when the same \emph{question} appears in both training and
test folds, models exploit question-specific shortcuts rather than genuine
answer understanding. We present an interpretable feature-fusion grader---a
NaN-native gradient-boosted head over lexical, semantic, and rubric-coverage
branches---and, more importantly, a rigorous evaluation protocol built around
\textbf{grouped leave-questions-out} cross-validation. Across six heterogeneous
datasets we quantify the question-leakage inflation directly (up to
$+0.38$ macro-F1 on Powergrading), show that branch ablations identify the
linguistic branch as the workhorse, and validate model explanations against
human gold feedback on the SAF corpus. Our significance analysis uses a
\textbf{cluster bootstrap over questions} with family-wise correction; under
this honest protocol the head's gain is significant on four of six datasets and
\emph{not} significant on two---a conclusion the conventional item-level
bootstrap would have hidden. We release all code, splits, and reports.
\textbf{This is the Phase A (feature-only) version; a neural cross-encoder arm
is added in the extended version (Section~\ref{sec:future}).}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Short answer grading asks a model to score a free-text student response against a
question and (often) a reference answer. The task sits between paraphrase
detection and ordinal regression, and a deployed grader must be both accurate
\emph{and} trustworthy to a teacher. Two problems motivate this work.

First, \textbf{evaluation honesty}. Many ASAG corpora have no official
train/test split, so practitioners fall back on stratified $k$-fold CV. But if a
question's answers are split across folds, the model can memorise the
question's answer distribution---a shortcut that vanishes on genuinely new
questions. We show this inflation is large and dataset-dependent.

Second, \textbf{interpretability}. High-capacity encoders grade well but cannot
tell a teacher \emph{why} a mark was given. A feature-fusion grader whose inputs
are human-readable (overlap, negation, concept coverage) can, and gradient-boosted
trees admit exact per-feature attributions via TreeSHAP \cite{lundberg2020treeshap}.

We contribute: (1) a leakage-aware grouped leave-questions-out protocol and an
explicit audit of the inflation it removes; (2) an interpretable feature-fusion
head evaluated across six datasets spanning classification, ordinal, and
regression targets; (3) branch ablations isolating each feature family's
contribution; (4) an explainability validation against SAF human feedback; and
(5) a cluster-bootstrap significance analysis with Holm correction that revises
two ``significant'' results to non-significant.

\section{Related Work}
ASAG has moved from hand-engineered similarity features
\cite{mohler2011learning,burrows2015eras} through the SemEval-2013 shared task
\cite{dzikovska2013semeval} to neural and transformer scorers
\cite{riordan2017investigating,sung2019pretraining}. Interpretability in this
setting is comparatively under-explored; we use the model-agnostic SHAP framework
\cite{lundberg2017shap} in its exact tree form \cite{lundberg2020treeshap}.
Calibration of classifier confidence follows temperature scaling
\cite{guo2017calibration}. Our significance methodology follows the cluster
(block) bootstrap \cite{efron1994bootstrap} with Holm--Bonferroni family-wise
control \cite{holm1979simple}. The hybrid encoder arm (extended version) builds on
sentence \cite{reimers2019sbert} and disentangled-attention \cite{he2021deberta}
transformers.

\section{Method}
\paragraph{Two-view preprocessing.} Each answer is normalised twice: an
\emph{encoder view} (NFKC + whitespace only) for embeddings, and a \emph{feature
view} (lemmatised, lowercased, stopword-stripped, negators preserved) for the
linguistic features, plus a negation-scope variant that prefixes tokens inside a
negator's scope.

\paragraph{Feature branches.} Thirty-one interpretable features in three families:
\textbf{(A) semantic}---SBERT \cite{reimers2019sbert} cosine and aggregate
interaction statistics; \textbf{(B) linguistic}---lexical/content overlap,
TF-IDF, negation cues, named-entity overlap, length ratios; \textbf{(C)
rubric}---per-reference-sentence concept-coverage proxies.

\paragraph{Fusion head.} A NaN-native LightGBM \cite{ke2017lightgbm} regressor or
classifier per the dataset's task type; ordinal targets use a
round-and-clip regression head. Hyperparameters are tuned with Optuna
\cite{akiba2019optuna} on a dev split (or inner CV), never touching the test
split. Every learned result is paired with a trivial baseline
(majority/mean/median).

\section{Evaluation Protocol: Leakage-Aware CV}
\label{sec:protocol}
For datasets without an official split we use \textbf{grouped (leave-questions-out)
$k$-fold} via score-stratified \texttt{StratifiedGroupKFold} on \texttt{question\_id},
so no question spans train and test---an honest \emph{unseen-question} estimate.
Alongside it we report the legacy stratified $k$-fold as a \emph{seen-question
upper bound}; the gap between them is the leakage the grouped protocol removes
(Table~\ref{tab:leakage}). Powergrading and MIND-CA inflate by $\sim$84--103\%
relative under the leaky protocol.

\paragraph{Significance (cluster bootstrap + Holm).} We resample whole
\emph{questions} with replacement ($10{,}000$ replicates) so within-question
correlation is respected, and apply Holm--Bonferroni across the six tests
(Table~\ref{tab:sig}, Fig.~\ref{fig:sig}). The gain is significant on SemEval,
ASAP-SAS, Mohler, and MIND-CA, and \textbf{not} significant on Powergrading
($p=0.146$; only 20 questions) or SAF. An item-level bootstrap would have called
Powergrading significant---the cluster bootstrap is what prevents that error.

\paragraph{Ablations.} Removing the linguistic branch (B) collapses every dataset;
the semantic branch (A) adds little and the rubric branch (C) adds essentially
nothing to accuracy (Fig.~\ref{fig:abl}). Negation cues give a small consistent
gain, validating the negation-scope preprocessing.

\paragraph{Explainability and faithfulness.} Global TreeSHAP rankings agree with
the gain-based importance ranking (Spearman $\rho \in [0.65, 0.999]$). On SAF,
the interpretable signals rise monotonically across the human verdict
Incorrect$\rightarrow$Partial$\rightarrow$Correct (semantic cosine strongest,
$\rho \approx 0.26$, AUC $\approx 0.62$), quantitatively linking model
attributions to teacher judgements (Fig.~\ref{fig:saf}). Notably, the rubric
branch is \emph{faithful} (its values track grades and push predictions in the
right direction) yet near-zero accuracy-additive---an explanation layer that does
not trade accuracy for transparency.

\paragraph{Calibration and robustness.} Post-hoc temperature scaling
\cite{guo2017calibration}, fit on a held-out validation slice, roughly halves
SemEval's expected calibration error ($0.122 \rightarrow 0.059$) and reduces
Powergrading's severe miscalibration ($0.613 \rightarrow 0.334$,
Fig.~\ref{fig:cal}). Perturbation tests show the grader is most sensitive to
negation flips, as intended, but brittle to paraphrase token drop---a documented
limitation.

\section{Limitations}
The semantic branch uses a single lightweight bi-encoder, which likely
under-represents its potential contribution (addressed by the cross-encoder arm
in the extended version). Mohler and ASAP-SAS are subsets of their original
corpora, so we report them as internal numbers only and make no head-to-head
claim against published results (no comparison in this paper is split- and
metric-matched; see \texttt{reports/phase3/published\_comparison.md}). SAF's
unseen-question performance collapses to near-zero correlation; we therefore
treat SAF as an explainability case study rather than an accuracy result. Dataset
sizes are modest, bounding any deep-learning claim.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
We presented an interpretable ASAG grader and, centrally, a leakage-aware
evaluation that quantifies and removes question-level shortcuts, with significance
established by a cluster bootstrap under family-wise correction. The honest
picture is that hand-engineered linguistic features carry the model, explanations
are faithful, and two apparent wins do not survive rigorous testing.
\textbf{Future work (extended version):} a fine-tuned DeBERTa cross-encoder
\cite{he2021deberta} whose out-of-fold signals are fused into the same
interpretable head, evaluated under identical grouped folds, with
transformer-only and feature-only arms reported side by side; nested CV for the
$k$-fold datasets; and widening ASAP-SAS to all ten prompts.
